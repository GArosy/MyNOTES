Philosophers in Architects

Louis Kahn (February 20, 1901 – March 17, 1974) is one of the most famous American architects in the 20th century. He is best known for his combination of modernism and the massiness and dignity of ancient relics. Although he didn't form a recognizable personal style until his early 50s and died at the age of 73, in just two decades, Kang became a master of the pantheon of modernist architects, where great architects such as Le Corbusier and Mies van der Rohe shine.

Kahn may be classified as late modernism and belongs to one of them with great influence. During his trip to Europe in 1928, he became more interested in medieval architecture, such as castles and sieges. In his later life, his understanding of ancient architecture evolved into the depth and stability of his own architecture, which echoed with Alvar Aalto's brick architecture and brutalist in Corbusier's later works in many aspects. Speaking of Corbusier, he left his early mechanical aesthetics after the 1950s. Kahn's most famous project is probably four freedoms Park in New York, which was finally completed in 2012 after his death.

His personal impression on the world is equally mysterious. His sometimes profound but always profound understanding of architecture makes him often described as a "mystic" or "master". His complicated private life inspired his son to shoot the documentary "my architect" for him in 2003, which won an Oscar nomination. In the film, Philip Johnson, his contemporary, said, "Kahn is his own artist. Compared with me, he is free."

Kahn died in 1974. After returning to New York from a site visit to the Indian Institute of management, Kahn had a heart attack in the men's toilet at the Pennsylvania Station. Although widely praised, his studio was in deep debt at that time, and his family did not receive notice of his death for two days due to poor communication between the police departments in New York and Philadelphia. But Kahn's non ceremonial end of life brought luck in misfortune: he found a design of Roosevelt Island in New York in memory of Franklin Delano Roosevelt in his briefcase. Nearly four years later, these drawings contributed to Kahn's final project, the four freedoms park.
